### Science Domain (Biology/Animal Cognition – Informative/Expository; Multi-Group Experiments)

- **Criterion B:** Reputable (Nature portfolio), stylistically clear/grammatical, unbiased (pure empirical), diverse French research team (CNRS/INRAE animal cognition experts). Grade 9–12 level; no jargon overload.  

**Sample Reading Passage Excerpt (432 words – Methods/Results focus):**  
Domestic hens adapt their foraging strategies based on available information about food location and environmental constraints, as demonstrated in a sloped-tubes task where hens searched for mealworm rewards in one of two hollow tubes. The central idea is that hens selectively modify their search behavior to optimize food retrieval, shifting from random or biased choices to exclusion when information is limited and risks are high.  

In the Introduction, foraging constitutes up to 60% of hens' behavioral time budget, and while exploratory foragers might search randomly, domestic hens adapt strategies to environmental cues like proximal (e.g., cup color) and distal (e.g., relative positions) information. The study adapted the sloped-tubes task to test hypotheses under free-choice conditions (no penalty for incorrect choices), expecting hens to directly approach visible rewards and possibly choose randomly without visibility. A side-biased strategy emerged when rewards were non-visible, with hens favoring the right tube, leading to chance-level performance (e.g., 48.26% success in standard trials). This suggests passive, non-goal-oriented behavior without information, though contrafreeloading alternatives were considered.  

Methods outlined the study design: 12 adult Isa Brown hens (1.5-3 years old) were habituated to a 1.5m x 1.2m arena with a starting box, then trained to find mealworms in tubes or a square box. Groups were divided into two cohorts of six: one tested container changes (tubes to square box) under free-choice; the other added constraints (forced-choice, where unbaited tubes became inaccessible post-choice, increasing non-reward risk). Variables included trial configurations—control (both tubes visible), visible probe (baited visible), non-visible probe (unbaited visible), standard (both non-visible)—with visibility as key information, and constraints like wall barriers. Hypothesis testing involved binomial tests for individual performance against 50% chance, Wilcoxon tests for group levels, and generalized linear models for side biases. Data collection focused on first-tube choice as success metric.  

Results showed hens differentiated visible (92.36% success in control trials) from non-visible conditions under free-choice, but in non-visible probe trials, performance was at chance (43.05%), with a right-side bias in non-visible setups (e.g., Kruskal-Wallis Chi² = 17.68, p < 0.001). Container changes (square box) did not alter strategies (29.17% success in non-visible probes, chance-level). However, forced-choice constraints boosted adaptation: four of six hens learned exclusion (choosing the non-visible tube after seeing emptiness in the other), achieving 83.5% success in later sessions (e.g., 94.5% for one hen over 18 trials, binomial p < 0.05). Group performance improved from 42.59% to above-chance levels, with effect sizes indicating strong strategy shifts (Cohen’s d via pairwise comparisons). Control trials confirmed no perceptual biases (100% success when visible).  

This evidence supports hens' cognitive flexibility: they use direct visibility for efficient gains but adapt to exclusion under risk, optimizing based on information and constraints.
