# SAT Reading & Writing Content Search Prompt

## Objective
Identify and evaluate high-quality articles, essays, blogs, and books suitable for generating SAT Reading & Writing test questions for the March 2026 test cycle. The sourced material must align with the specific refinements outlined in the 2026 Pattern Change guide, emphasizing critical thinking, scientific literacy, and logical clarity.

## Search Strategy Parameters

### 1. Publication Date & Relevance
*   **Target Range:** 2015 – Present (Priority given to 2020–2025 for contemporary relevance).
*   **Focus:** Recent scientific discoveries, modern literary fiction, current historical analysis, and contemporary humanities discussions.
*   **Avoid:** Outdated references, debunked theories, or culturally insensitive material.

### 2. Content Domains & Thematic Diversity
Source material must cover the following four domains:

*   **Literature (Fiction/Prose):**
    *   *Focus:* Modern and classic literary fiction from diverse global authors.
    *   *Key Elements:* Character development, plot structure, narrative voice, and thematic depth.
    *   *2026 Shift:* Look for texts that allow for nuanced inference questions (e.g., recognizing equivalent meanings expressed differently).

*   **History / Social Studies:**
    *   *Focus:* Economics, sociology, political science, psychology, and historical analysis.
    *   *Key Elements:* Arguments based on evidence, analysis of trends, and cause-and-effect reasoning.
    *   *2026 Shift:* Prioritize texts that discuss study designs or evaluate conflicting historical interpretations.

*   **Science:**
    *   *Focus:* Biology, chemistry, physics, astronomy, and earth science.
    *   *Key Elements:* Experimental descriptions, data interpretation, and hypothesis testing.
    *   *2026 Shift:* **Crucial:** Find texts detailing experiments with multi-group scenarios. Material must support questions about variable testing, study design evaluation, and inferring conclusions from limited data.

*   **Humanities:**
    *   *Focus:* Art, music, philosophy, and cultural criticism.
    *   *Key Elements:* Rhetorical analysis, aesthetic theory, and critical commentary.
    *   *2026 Shift:* Texts should use "solid academic words" rather than obscure archaic vocabulary.

### 3. Text Specifications
*   **Reading Passages:** 400–450 words.
    *   *Complexity:* Grades 9–12 reading level (Lexile 1050L–1335L).
    *   *Structure:* Must have a clear central idea, supporting evidence, and logical progression.
*   **Writing Passages:** 150–200 words.
    *   *Complexity:* Grades 9–12 reading level.
    *   *Structure:* Must contain opportunities for testing transitions, rhetorical synthesis, and sentence boundaries.

### 4. Rhetorical Modes
*   **Argumentative:** Persuasive essays, opinion pieces, and scientific arguments.
*   **Narrative:** Fiction excerpts, memoirs, and personal essays.
*   **Informative/Expository:** Scientific reports, historical accounts, and explanatory texts.

### 5. Author Demographics & Copyright
*   **Diversity:** Prioritize authors from diverse racial, ethnic, gender, and geographic backgrounds.
*   **Copyright:** Ensure materials are either public domain, Creative Commons licensed, or available for fair use educational adaptation.

## Evaluation Metrics for Sourcing

### Criterion A: Alignment with 2026 Skills
1.  **Command of Evidence (Textual & Quantitative):**
    *   Does the text provide specific data points or textual evidence that can be cited?
    *   *Check:* Can a question be framed where incorrect options sound reasonable but fail due to small inaccuracies in the text?
2.  **Inference & Interpretation:**
    *   Does the text allow for "paraphrasing recognition" (e.g., "foraging behavior" -> "hunting for food")?
    *   Is the meaning implicit enough to require deep reading but explicit enough to have one indisputable answer?
3.  **Words in Context:**
    *   Does the text use high-utility academic vocabulary (Tier 2 words) in clear contexts?
    *   *Avoid:* Texts with overly niche jargon or archaic language.
4.  **Expression of Ideas (Transitions & Rhetoric):**
    *   Does the text have complex logical relationships (contrast, cause-and-effect) suitable for testing nuanced transitions (e.g., "contrary to the phenomenon")?
    *   Are there sentences that can be modified to test "dangling modifiers" or logical flow?

### Criterion B: Quality & Credibility
*   **Source Authority:** Is the publisher reputable (e.g., major university press, established literary journal, peer-reviewed science journal)?
*   **Clarity:** Is the writing stylistically strong and grammatically standard (unless testing specific rhetorical deviations)?
*   **Bias Check:** Is the text free from political, religious, or cultural bias that would distract from the assessment goal?
