### Understand the SAT Reading and Writing Domains and Skills

Refer to the SAT Reading and Writing guide provided. Ensure the questions align with the **four content domains** and their respective skills. Based on Bluebook Test 11, the March 2026 SAT reflects a thoughtful refinement in *how* these domains are tested — rewarding deeper critical thinking, scientific literacy, and logical clarity over surface-level recall or grammar trivia.

---

- **Information and Ideas**: Measures comprehension, analysis, and reasoning skills and knowledge, as well as the ability to locate, interpret, evaluate, and integrate information and ideas from texts and informational graphics (tables, bar graphs, and line graphs).
  - Central Ideas and Details
  - Inferences
  - Command of Evidence

  > **2026 Shift:** Students are increasingly asked to interpret experiments, evaluate study design, and infer conclusions from limited data. Questions may present multi-group experimental scenarios where students must identify what variable is being tested and what the results imply. Inference questions now reward paraphrasing recognition — if a passage references "foraging behavior," the correct answer may refer to "marine animals hunting for food." Surface-level scanning strategies don't work; students must recognize equivalent meanings expressed differently. For Command of Evidence (bullet-point questions), students must return to the bullet points and confirm that each answer choice is fully supported by the data provided — many incorrect options sound reasonable but contain small inaccuracies.

---

- **Craft and Structure**: Measures the comprehension, vocabulary, analysis, synthesis, and reasoning skills and knowledge needed to understand and use high-utility words and phrases in context, evaluate texts rhetorically, and make connections between topically related texts.
  - Words in Context
  - Text Structure and Purpose
  - Cross-Text Connections

  > **2026 Shift:** Vocabulary appears more practical than intimidating — the test includes solid academic words but avoids extremely rare or archaic vocabulary. The emphasis has shifted toward understanding meaning in context rather than memorizing obscure word lists. For Transitions, rather than simply choosing between words like "however" or "similarly," students may need to select nuanced phrases such as "contrary to the phenomenon" or "undermining this explanation" — answering correctly now requires understanding not just that two ideas contrast, but exactly what is being contradicted.

---

- **Expression of Ideas**: Measures the ability to revise texts to improve the effectiveness of written expression and to meet specific rhetorical goals.
  - Rhetorical Synthesis
  - Transitions

  > **2026 Shift:** Grammar questions focus more on clarity, logical flow, and effective expression. Dangling modifiers appear more prominently — sentences that technically follow punctuation rules but fail logically are being tested more directly. This reflects a shift toward real-world writing clarity over narrow technical rules.

---

- **Standard English Conventions**: Measures the ability to edit text to conform to core conventions of Standard English sentence structure, usage, and punctuation.
  - Boundaries
  - Form, Structure, and Sense

  > **2026 Shift:** Bluebook Test 11 suggests a noticeable reduction in highly technical punctuation traps. Previously, students needed to master narrow colon or semicolon rules that felt uniquely "SAT-specific." These appear less frequently. The focus has moved toward sentence-level logic and structure rather than obscure punctuation edge cases.

---

**Overall 2026 Testing Posture:** The March 2026 SAT does not represent a dramatic overhaul, but it does reflect a thoughtful refinement. The exam increasingly rewards students who think critically, reason scientifically, and communicate clearly. Question writers are moving away from isolated skill testing toward integrated, multi-layered questions that require genuine comprehension rather than test-taking tricks.
